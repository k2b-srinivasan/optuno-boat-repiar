Miami Boat Hurricane Prep 2026: Real Costs & What to Do Now

May 28, 2026 · 10 min read · By Boat Repair Miami

Atlantic hurricane season officially runs June 1 through November 30. For Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach boat owners, that means nearly half the year with your vessel potentially in harm's way. The question we get asked most often starting in May is simple: "What should I do with my boat, and what's it actually going to cost?"

This guide breaks down both the options and the real numbers — based on what we see working with boat owners throughout South Florida every season.

Option 1: Haul-Out and Dry Storage

Hauling your boat onto dry land before a named storm is the gold standard for protection. A boat on the hard — strapped, blocked, and properly supported — has dramatically better odds of surviving a major storm than one left in the water.

What It Costs

  • Haul-out fee: $8–$18 per foot depending on the yard. A 30-footer = $240–$540.
  • Dry storage (per month): $12–$25 per foot. For a 30-footer, that's $360–$750/month. Pre-season arrangements are often required — yards fill fast before named storms.
  • Re-launch: Similar to haul-out. Budget the same again.
  • Bottom cleaning and antifouling after: $500–$2,500 depending on size and condition.

Total cost for a single haul-and-store event (30' boat, 4 weeks): roughly $1,500–$4,000 all-in.

Option 2: Storm-Safe Dock or Slip Preparation

If haul-out isn't possible or practical, a properly prepared dock can provide reasonable protection — with the right lines, chafe protection, and fender arrangement. This is riskier than hauling out, but many boat owners successfully ride out Cat 1–2 storms this way.

What Proper Dock Prep Involves

  • Dock lines: Double up with spring lines, bow and stern lines, and breast lines at a minimum. Use lines at least ¾" diameter (or larger for heavy boats). Budget $150–$400 for quality dock lines if you don't have them.
  • Chafe guards: Every line that touches a cleat, piling, or chock needs chafe protection. $30–$100.
  • Fender arrangement: Cover the entire waterside of the hull at multiple heights. Extra fenders: $50–$200.
  • Remove canvas, covers, and loose items: This is free but critical. Biminis, seat cushions, cockpit tables — anything that can catch wind comes off.
  • Fuel up: Full tanks add ballast and stability.

Professional dock tie-down service: Many mobile mechanics (including our team) offer pre-storm preparation visits — running the lines, arranging fenders, and advising on your specific dock situation. Budget $200–$600 for this service. It's money well spent.

Option 3: Canal or Creek Anchoring

Some boat owners anchor in a protected canal or creek — a traditional South Florida practice. This can be effective, but it requires the right anchor gear, excellent scope, and ideally a bridle setup. It also requires you to monitor the boat or have someone check on it during and after the storm.

We don't typically recommend this for boats over 30' or for anyone without solid anchoring experience and quality ground tackle.

Insurance Considerations

This is where preparation really pays for itself. Most marine insurance policies have hurricane clauses that specify what you must do before a storm — haul-out requirements, named storm exclusions, or lay-up conditions. Failing to comply can void your coverage.

Read your policy carefully. If you're unsure, call your broker before June 1. Some policies require haul-out if a storm track is within a certain radius of your location. Others require you to move the vessel out of a named storm zone.

Pre-Season Checklist

  • Read your marine insurance hurricane clause — know what's required
  • Book a haul-out slot at your preferred yard early (before June)
  • Have your bilge pump tested and operational
  • Inspect dock lines and replace any that are worn or have chafe damage
  • Take inventory of fenders and order extras if needed
  • Remove all canvas, cushions, and loose gear during storm watches
  • Photograph your boat and all equipment for insurance documentation
  • Have a marine mechanic inspect through-hulls and seacocks
  • Confirm your emergency contact and marina/yard contact information

Post-Storm Damage Assessment

If your boat was in the water during a storm, or if you suspect damage even from a near-miss, a professional post-storm inspection is essential. Osmotic blistering, hull stress, electrical corrosion from flooding, and engine damage from debris or submersion may not be visible but can show up weeks later — outside your insurance claim window.

Our team conducts post-storm damage assessments throughout Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties. Call us as soon as it's safe to access your boat.
